## About Cantonese Cuisine

Guangzhou is the birthplace and capital of Cantonese (Yue) cuisine, one of China's Eight Great Culinary Traditions. If you love food, Guangzhou is a pilgrimage destination. The Cantonese saying "吃在广州" (Chī zài Guǎngzhōu) means "To eat in Guangzhou" - reflecting the city's obsession with exceptional food.

## Characteristics of Cantonese Food

- **Fresh ingredients**: Emphasis on quality and freshness
- **Light seasoning**: Let natural flavors shine
- **Subtle flavors**: Less spicy and oily than other Chinese cuisines
- **Variety**: "If it has four legs and it's not a table, Cantonese will eat it"
- **Soups**: Clear, delicate, slow-cooked
- **Seafood**: Abundant and essential
- **Steaming**: Preferred cooking method to preserve flavors

## Morning Tea (Yum Cha - 饮茶)

### The Ultimate Guangzhou Experience

**What It Is**:
- Traditional Cantonese brunch culture
- Dim sum served with tea
- Social gathering, not rushed
- Can last hours

**When**: Usually 7:00 AM - 2:00 PM (peak: 9-11 AM)

**How It Works**:
1. Arrive at tea house or restaurant
2. Choose your tea (usually by table)
3. Order dim sum dishes (small plates)
4. Pay per basket/plate
5. Enjoy leisurely with tea

### Essential Dim Sum Dishes to Try

#### Must-Order Classics

**Har Gow (虾饺)** $$
- Crystal shrimp dumplings
- Translucent wrapper
- Sweet fresh shrimp filling
- Judge quality by wrapper thinness

**Siu Mai (烧卖)** $$
- Open-topped pork dumplings
- Pork and shrimp
- Topped with roe or carrot
- Golden yellow color

**Char Siu Bao (叉烧包)** $
- BBQ pork buns
- Fluffy steamed bun
- Sweet-savory pork filling
- Both steamed and baked versions

**Cheong Fun (肠粉)** $
- Rice noodle rolls
- Filled with shrimp, pork, or vegetables
- Delicate, silky texture
- Light soy sauce

**Egg Tart (蛋挞)** $
- Portuguese-influenced pastry
- Flaky crust
- Smooth egg custard filling
- Best served warm

#### Adventurous Options

**Phoenix Claws (凤爪)** $
- Chicken feet
- Braised until tender
- Black bean sauce
- Surprisingly delicious

**Spare Ribs with Black Bean (豉汁排骨)** $$
- Steamed pork ribs
- Fermented black beans
- Garlicky and savory

**Tripe (牛百叶)** $
- Honeycomb beef tripe
- Ginger and scallions
- Chewy texture

**Lo Mai Gai (糯米鸡)** $$
- Sticky rice wrapped in lotus leaf
- Chicken, mushroom, sausage inside
- Filling and flavorful

**Dim Sum Icon**
- Modern interpretation
- Stylish setting

#### Local Favorites ($$)

**Diandude (点都德)**
- Local chain
- Excellent value
- Always crowded (good sign)
- No reservations, expect wait
- ¥60-100 per person

**Taotaoju (陶陶居)**
- Historic (1880s)
- Renovated, touristy but good

**Lin Heung Tea House (莲香楼)**
- Old-school, chaotic
- Pushcarts (rare now)
- Locals fighting for seats
- Authentic experience
- ¥40-70 per person
- **Note**: Cash only, very crowded, no English

## Signature Cantonese Dishes

### Beyond Dim Sum

#### Roast Meats (烧味)

**Char Siu (叉烧)** - BBQ Pork
- Sweet and savory
- Charred edges
- Served over rice or alone
- ¥30-50 for a plate

**Roast Goose (烧鹅)** 
- Crispy skin, tender meat
- Cantonese specialty
- Better than Peking duck (locals say)
- ¥60-150 depending on portion

**Siu Yuk (烧肉)** - Crispy Pork Belly
- Crackling skin
- Tender meat
- Served chopped
- ¥40-80 for a plate

#### Seafood

**Steamed Fish (清蒸鱼)**
- Fresh fish (often live-selected)
- Steamed with ginger and scallions
- Light soy sauce
- Delicate and fresh
- ¥60-200+ depending on fish

**Salt and Pepper Squid (椒盐鱿鱼)**
- Crispy fried squid
- Spicy salt seasoning
- Garlic and chilies
- ¥40-80

**Typhoon Shelter Crab (避风塘炒蟹)**
- Stir-fried crab
- Crispy garlic and chili
- Messy but delicious
- ¥80-200+

#### Soups

**Double-Boiled Soups (炖汤)**
- Slow-cooked for hours
- Clear, delicate broth
- Various medicinal ingredients
- Health benefits emphasized
- ¥30-80 per serving

#### Rice & Noodles

**Wonton Noodles (云吞面)**
- Thin egg noodles
- Shrimp wontons
- Clear broth
- Simple perfection
- ¥15-30

**Claypot Rice (煲仔饭)**
- Rice cooked in clay pot
- Toppings: sausage, chicken, pork
- Crispy rice at bottom (best part)
- ¥25-45

**Fried Rice Noodles (干炒牛河)**
- Beef chow fun
- Wide rice noodles
- Beef and bean sprouts
- "Wok hei" (breath of wok) essential
- ¥25-40

## Dietary Considerations

### Vegetarian

**Challenges**:
- Cantonese food very seafood/meat-focused
- Broths often contain meat
- Oyster sauce common

**Options**:
- Buddhist vegetarian restaurants (素食)
- Specify "我吃素" (wǒ chī sù) - I'm vegetarian
- Vegetables dishes (confirm no meat)
- Some dim sum options (veggie dumplings)

### Vegan

**More Difficult**:
- Dairy rare, but eggs common
- Many dim sum have egg
- Stick to Buddhist vegetarian places

### Allergies

**Common Allergens**:
- Shellfish (everywhere)
- Peanuts (less common than Sichuan)
- Eggs (in many dishes)

**Phrases**:
- 我对...过敏 (wǒ duì...guò mǐn) - I'm allergic to...

### Spice Level

**Good News**:
- Cantonese food NOT spicy
- Mild seasoning
- Natural flavors emphasized
- Excellent for those who don't like heat

## Dining Etiquette

### Tea Culture
- Tap two fingers when someone pours tea (thanks)
- Signal refill by leaving lid ajar
- Don't pour your own first

### Ordering
- Order multiple dishes to share
- Rice ordered separately
- Ask for recommendations
- Point at pictures

### Eating
- Chopsticks only (usually)
- Bones on table or small plate OK
- Slurping acceptable
- Leave some food (shows hospitality)

### Paying
- Ask for bill: "买单" (mǎi dān)
- Usually pay at register
- Split bills unusual
- Cash or mobile payment most common

## Food Safety

- Guangzhou food generally very safe
- High turnover at popular spots
- Fresh ingredients emphasized
- Stick to busy restaurants
- Bottled water
- Cooked food safer than raw
